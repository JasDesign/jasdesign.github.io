Sensory Design for Urban Greenery

BACKGROUND

Public health is at growing risk from sensory overload of urban condition such as pollution, noise, overcrowding and stress. It’s scientifically proven that urban ecological spaces reduce stress and the psychological ramifications of city chaos for people. However, most U.S. cities lack interactive open green spaces. Most plant designs are merely wallpaper, lack aesthetic coordination, and fail to connect people to nature in a meaningful or therapeutic way to relieve stress. As a result, plants’ roles in urban design are underused or are afterthoughts, and a market remains to develop the relationship between plant-based sensory design and urban populations in a limited space.

PROBLEM

The current green space designs lack aesthetic value and fail to combat urban stress through a multi-sensory approach. Public health is at growing risk from the stressed living in concrete jungle.

Sensory Design with Healing Plants

Our perception of and appreciation for urban space is largely determined by the quality of the green space. The quality of greenery can promote health and well-being by exposing our brain to the right balances of stimuli in a way that creates positive emotions and experiences. Thus, plants plays a major role in open space in a sensibility of nature, relax and health. Especially, some plants rich in tactility and scents, such as rosemary and lavender, are therapeutic beneficial to people with stress and sensory impairment. According to my volunteer experience in a horticultural therapy center, those non-visual sensory elements of plants encouraged people engaging with nature and uplifted their moods.

Sensory design considers the nature of human beings to arrive at integrated designs in which all senses are activated: sight, hearing, taste, touch, and smell. Those senses fuel people’s emotions, wielding the power to persuade, relax and heal. Sensory design provides a positive stimulation experience effects in emotion, cognition and well-being.

Therefore, to combine sensory design with healing plants into urban infrastructure will optimize the horticultural therapeutic benefit and soothe people away from urban stress. Connecting people with nature through an interactive and healing experience will also promote the utilization of urban greenery.
